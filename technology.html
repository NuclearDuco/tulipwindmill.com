The Root Challenge

Friction is the enemy of small turbines

Conventional small vertical-axis turbines use standard ball bearings to support the rotor's full weight. At the low wind speeds found on urban rooftops, bearing friction consumes a disproportionate share of available torque. The turbine stalls — or never starts at all.

Our engineers traced the problem to its root: offload the axial load and friction drops to near zero, letting the turbine respond to the gentlest breeze. The solution was hiding in physics — Ring magnets placed in mutual repulsion.

The Mechanism

Three forces working as one

Our rotor combines passive magnetic levitation, aerodynamic Savonius drag, and Darrieus lift — each force reinforcing the others so the turbine spins in almost any breeze and loses virtually no energy to friction.

🧲

Magnet Repulsion

Two ring magnets — one on the rotor, one on the fixed frame — face each other with the same magnetic pole. The repulsion force cushions the rotor axially without any mechanical contact.

🌀

Savonius Starting Torque

The inner concave face of each carbon blade captures wind by drag, providing instant torque at even the lowest wind speeds. The turbine is fully self-starting — no electronics, no kickstart motor needed.

⚡

Darrieus Aerodynamic Lift

The outer leading edge forms a NACA-inspired airfoil. As wind flows across it, lift is generated — just like an aircraft wing. At higher speeds this effect takes over, dramatically boosting efficiency.

🔩

Ceramic Stability Bearing

Earnshaw's theorem means purely passive levitation is unstable. A single ceramic ball bearing — carrying only ~10 % of rotor weight — provides radial control without reintroducing meaningful friction.

🪶

Carbon Fibre Blades

The three helix blades are moulded from aerospace-grade carbon fibre and finished in deep purple — maximising stiffness while minimising rotational mass, so the magnetic system has less work to do.

🏭

Chimney-Ready System

The entire assembly mounts directly onto standard chimney stacks at up to 1.2 m height — no permits, no new foundations — accessing cleaner airflow above rooftop turbulence.

Engineering Detail

The magnet specification

Every element of the magnet pair has been chosen to balance holding force, stability and long-term reliability. The carbon fibre rotor keeps total rotational mass low — which means smaller magnets, lower cost, and a lighter stator structure.

The air gap between the rings is maintained at 2–4 mm. Within this range the repulsion force offloads the rotor while tolerating small manufacturing variations — critical for a product installed on a rooftop by a single technician.
